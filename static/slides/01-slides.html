Learning from Data

The following are the basic requirements for statistical learning:

  1. A pattern exists.
  2. This pattern is not easily expressed in a closed mathematical form.
  3. You have data.

Formalization

We think of our outcome-of-interest as a response or target that we wish to predict or wish to learn something about.

We generically refer to the response as YYY.

Other aspects of the data are known as features, inputs, predictors, or regressors. We call one of these XiX_iXi​.

We can refer to the input vector collectively as

X=[x11x12x21x22x31x32⋮⋮]X = \begin{bmatrix} x_{11} & x_{12}\\ x_{21} & x_{22} \\ x_{31} & x_{32} \\ \vdots & \vdots \end{bmatrix}X=⎣⎢⎢⎢⎢⎡​x11​x21​x31​⋮​x12​x22​x32​⋮​⎦⎥⎥⎥⎥⎤​

We are seeking some unknow function that maps XXX to YYY.

Put another way, we are seeking to explain YYY as follows:

Formalization

We call the function f:X→Yf : \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}f:X→Y the target function.

The target function is always unknown. It is the object of learning.

Methodology:

  • Observe data (x1,y1)…(xN,yN)(x_1, y_1) \dots (x_N, y_N)(x1​,y1​)…(xN​,yN​).
  • Use some algorithm to approximate fff.
  • Produce final hypothesis function g≈fg \approx fg≈f.
  • Evaluate how well ggg approximates fff; iterate as needed.

Why estimate an unknown function?

With a good estimate of fff we can make predictions of YYY at new points X=xX = xX=x.

We can understand which components of X=(X1,X2,…,Xm)X = (X_1, X_2, \dots , X_m)X=(X1​,X2​,…,Xm​) are important in explaining YYY , and which are (potentially) irrelevant.

  • e.g., GDP and yearsindustrialized have a big impact on emissions, but hydroutilization typically does not.

Depending on the complexity of fff, we may be able to meaningfully understand how each component of XXX affects YYY.

(But we should be careful about assigning causal interpretations; more on this later)

The Parable of the Marbles

Within this bag of marbles are ♣\clubsuit♣ and ♢\diamondsuit♢ marbles

We are going to pick a sample of nnn marbles (with replacement).

The Parable of the Marbles

  • Let μ\muμ be the objective probability to pick a ♣\clubsuit♣.
  • Let ν\nuν be fraction of ♣\clubsuit♣ marbles in the sample.

Question: Can we say anything about μ\muμ (outside the data) after observing ν\nuν (the data)?

  • No. It is possible for the sample to be all ♣\clubsuit♣ marbles and the bag to be ≈♢\approx \diamondsuit≈♢. Consider a sample composed of  ♣ ♣ ♣ ♣ ♣~\clubsuit~\clubsuit~\clubsuit~\clubsuit~\clubsuit ♣ ♣ ♣ ♣ ♣. We can never rule out the possibility of drawing ♢\diamondsuit♢!

Question: Then why do we do polling (e.g. to predict the outcome of the presidential election)?

  • The bad case is possible, but not probable.

Outside the Data

Put another way, since fff is unknown, it can take on any value outside the data, no matter how large the data.

  • This is called No Free Lunch.

You cannot know anything for sure about fff outside the data without making assumptions.

Is there any hope to know anything about fff outside the data set without making assumptions about fff?

Yes, if we are willing to give up the "for sure."

Hoeffding's Inequality

Hoeffding's Inequality states, loosely, that ν\nuν cannot be too far from μ\muμ.

Theorem (Hoeffding's Inequality)

P[∣ν−μ∣>ϵ]≤2e−2ϵ2n\mathbb{P} \left [ | \nu - \mu | > \epsilon \right ] \leq 2 e^{-2\epsilon^2 n}P[∣ν−μ∣>ϵ]≤2e−2ϵ2n

ν≈μ\nu \approx \muν≈μ is called probably approximately correct (PAC-learning)
